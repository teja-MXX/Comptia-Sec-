# 1. What is a Risk Register?

A Risk Register (also called a _risk log_) is a structured document that records:

- each identified risk
    
- what that risk means
    
- how likely it is
    
- how severe it is
    
- what actions must be taken
    
- who is responsible for it
    
- how it will be monitored
    

It may also resemble a risk matrix / heat map, since impact + likelihood determine the severity.

A risk register ensures:

- visibility of risks
    
- accountability
    
- prioritization of mitigation
    
- consistent communication between teams

# 3. Risk Appetite & Risk Tolerance

Before assigning final risk levels, you must understand these two concepts:

---

## Risk Tolerance

Risk tolerance = maximum risk the organization is willing to accept without taking action.

If the risk falls within tolerance:

- no countermeasures
    
- no mitigation effort
    
- the organization accepts the possibility
    

Examples:

- Accepting minor delays
    
- Accepting minor cost changes
    
- Accepting low-impact cybersecurity alerts
    

Tolerance = â€œHow much risk can we live with?â€

---

## Risk Appetite

Risk appetite = how much and what type of risk an organization wants to pursue, in order to achieve goals.

Three types:

### 1. Expansionary Appetite

- High risk-taking
    
- High ambition
    
- Growth-focused industries  
    (e.g., startups, investment firms)
    

### 2. Conservative Appetite

- Low risk-taking
    
- Focus on stability  
    (e.g., banking, healthcare, government)
    

### 3. Neutral Appetite

- Balanced approach
    
- Take risks only when justified or controlled
    

Risk appetite guides:

- how risks are prioritized
    
- how aggressive or cautious decisions should be
    
- how mitigation budgets are allocated
    

---

# 4. Key Risk Indicators (KRIs)

KRIs = predictive metrics that give early warning signs of increasing risk exposure.

They are:

- measurable
    
- forward-looking
    
- tied to risk appetite
    
- tied to business objectives
    

KRIs help organizations act before the risk escalates.

Example (Banking):  
A KRI = number of loan defaults in a month.  
If defaults suddenly increase â†’ early signal of rising credit risk.

KRIs help teams:

- detect changes
    
- anticipate problems
    
- take proactive mitigation steps
    

---

# 5. Risk Owner

A Risk Owner is the person or group accountable for a specific risk.  
They are responsible for:

- monitoring the risk
    
- implementing mitigation actions
    
- updating the risk status
    
- communicating changes to stakeholders
    

Example:  
In a construction project, the Project Manager may own risks like:

- delays due to bad weather
    
- fluctuating material prices
    

The risk owner ensures:

- mitigation is applied
    
- controls are monitored
    
- the project stays aligned with objectives
